Modest Fashion

What is Modest Fashion?

Modest fashion is not as easily defined as sustainable fashion as it is very much up to interpretation. One's definition depends on many different factors, including where they live, what they believe, and what media they're exposed to.
To find out more about what people define modest fashion as, I interviewed people from different countries, different age groups, and different beliefs to see what they thought of when they heard the term "modest fashion", and these are some responses I got:

  • "Fashionable clothing that doesn't reveal too much"
  • "I would say modest fashion would be related to anything that has to do with hijabs... well anything that could link fashion and Muslim women in general"
  • "I think of wearing the hijab, with the arms, legs, and body completely covered (maybe showing the forearm or a bit of the calf)"
  • "When I hear modest fashion, I immediately think of girls wearing what they want and the comfortability they are seeking. Not for looks but for contentment"

A general trend was that most people associated modest fashion with Islam, especially with women in Islam, and it seemed as if the media thought the same. I asked the same people about whether or not they saw examples of modest clothing in the media, and where they saw it if they did:

  • "At the moment, I can't think of one, but I'm not totally educated on it"
  • "No, sadly"
  • "No"
  • "[This is] the first time I've heard about modest fashion ever, but when it comes to influencers, I know that the first time I saw a Muslim model on the runway was a girl during Zendaya's TommyxZendaya fashion show... It was the first time I actually saw something that every Muslim woman could wear while respecting the values of Islam being presented in a fashion show"
  • "Not explicity, no. Other than Halima (the girl from the TommyxZendaya fashion show), most celebrities who have come from my background have converted to the more western way of dressing, which leaves me unable to relate to them in that sense"

If people did not see much representation of modest fashion in the media, how then did they come to associate it with Islam?


While portrayal of modest fashion in the media is fairly limited, something that isn't is the portrayal of Muslim women in hijabs, niqabs, and burkas. This means that people and companies are basing modest fashion off of one specific group of people, a lot of which aren't even worried about fashion, since most women who wear burkas or niqabs are not wearing them for fashion.


While this might allow some Muslim women to feel as if they can relate to fashion, it leaves out many other people, both Muslim and non-Muslim, and male and female, who want to dress "modestly" for other reasons, such as personal preference for modesty, or just feeling self conscious when wearing something they don't find as modest.

In order to make sure this conclusion was shared by the people I interviewed, I asked them whether or not they thought that modest fashion should be presented in a more secular manner, or whether it was fine as it was. These were the responses:

  • "I think it should be presented in a more secular manner"
  • "If it's normal everyday street clothes that are just made to be more modest, I don't think I'd see a problem with that"
  • "I think it's fine as it is, but I'm not educated enough on the topic to have a valid opinion about it"
  • "100% more secular. Although I'm religious and do wear somewhat modest clothing because of that, I don't wear clothes as modest as most Muslims or hijabis, but I do like to dress more modestly than most western people. This means I'm stuck in the middle, which is why I think, if there wasn't some sort of 'taboo' around modest clothing, people would be more comfortable wearing what they please, without the fear of being judged for being either 'too modest' or 'not modest enough'."

For the last response, most people were fairly split, however many people shared the response that it should be more secular in order to allow for a larger group of people to feel involved.

While most brands that classify their clothing as modest would be aimed more towards hijabi women, there are some clothes in certain companies that some may view as more modest, such as:

  1. Maxi dresses
  2. Midi length dresses
  3. Maxi skirts
  4. Midi skirts
  5. Shirts
  6. Pantsuits
  7. Long sleeved tops

These are just some of the many options of "modest" clothing that are easily available in many stores, even if they are not marketed as such.

While modest fashion should be included more prominently in the media in a more secular manner, there are fashion brands that do have modest clothing. It is important however to market it as modest clothing in order to make it easier and more accessible for those who are looking for it.
